 - what you need to do to achieve higher order learning

You can apply interleaving to learning by reviewing information from multiple angles and perspectives. Research strongly shows that more interleaving produces better results. In addition, because you will become used to seeing the information in multiple ways, it will protect you from most **curveball exam questions**.

As you get better at encoding, you will rely less on retrieval. However, every learning system should always have at least some level of interleaved retrieval practice, even if your encoding is perfect! This is because the human brain always prunes information that isn’t used eventually

## What does higher-order learning feel like?

Learning happens in the brain. We can’t see it with our eyes. Therefore, it’s important that you mentally know what higher-order learning _feels_ like so that you can start monitoring if you’re using the right processes.

### **Higher-order learning**
Comparing, contrasting, evaluating, and forming networks and relationships. This feels challenging, mentally effortful, and potentially confusing. Learners will often go back and forth between their material and spend lots of time thinking deeply and asking questions.

### **Lower-order learning**
Rote-memorising, repeating, trying to understand information in isolation (without comparing and creating networks). This feels tedious, time-consuming, mentally straightforward, and potentially boring and sleep-inducing.|
## Interleaving techniques
- **Teach** an imaginary student
- Draw a **mindmap**
- Draw an image instead of using words to find a visual way of expressing information
- Answer practice questions
- Create your own challenging test questions
- Create a test question that puts what you’ve learned into a real-world context
- Take a difficult question that you found in a practice test and modify it so that the variables are different, or an extra step is added
- Form a study group and quiz each other – for some subjects you can even debate the topic, with one side trying to prove that the other person is missing a point or understanding it incorrectly




# Efficient learning Steps
## priming
- skim through the text you have to learn
- Read the headings
- Look at some diagrams and pictures
- Get a **general overview**
- **No details**

## learning event
- use flow app 😀
- Be attentive
- **Ask yourself a lot of questions about the new info - a lot!** Multiple times per minute! Why? How? (…)
### going through material
- read through and memorize stuff you understand (build a foundation + connections)
- Skip stuff you don‘t understand + make a note of what it was
- Repeat the process multiple times, fill the gaps
- Group information together
	- How does this relate to…
	- What’s the difference between… (an arm and a leg)
- Learn good note taking
	- Nonlinear note taking is key

## after the event
- first revision no later than 24h after first contact with the new study subject
- Ideally revise before going to sleep on the same day (**[[sleep dependent memory consolidation]]**)

## how to do revisions
- find a way to represent new info in a connected way (mindmap)
- Make notes as non-verbal as possible - words and sentences are a last measure
- Try delayed note taking (try to write down notes in a simplified way) - **better do that in your head** 😀
- Pre exam revision
	- Make this challenging
	- Use recall rather than recognition
	- Draw the ultrasound image
	- Use different methods for repeated revisions - **(imaginarily) teach what you want to revise**
